Digital Divide Analysis

Project Description

With the rise of the digital economy, an important and evolving topic has been the digital divide. It was first granted international exposure and brought to the forefront when it began appearing in several United Nations reports, and has become a critical concern for organizations, policymakers, and scholars across various fields. Digital divide refers to the gap between people who have adequate access to information communication technology (ICT) and people who have poor or no access to ICT.

Traditionally, much of the discourse around the digital divide has framed the issue in binary terms: the presence or absence of internet coverage. While this perspective has been useful in establishing basic access goals, it obscures significant variations in the quality of internet access, including speed, reliability, and bandwidth.

Despite this recognition, current empirical approaches remain constrained. Most existing metrics are area-based, measuring coverage over a geographic extent without accounting for the distribution of populations within those areas. To address this gap, this report proposes a novel, population-based measurement index to capture the evolving nature of internet quality.

Beyond measurement, this report also explores predictive modeling techniques to fill existing data gaps. In regions where high-resolution internet speed data are unavailable or unreliable, can machine learning generate accurate predictions based on geospatial indicators? These secondary research questions assess methodological rigor and practical applicability.

Data Source

Full details of Ookla, Meta, Kontur, and World Bank datasets (see project notes).

Methodology

Internet speed metrics are aggregated from zoom-16 tiles into zoom-14 for efficiency, centroids assigned to World Bank boundaries, then weighted averages and medians computed.

About the Project

This project is part of a research initiative on digital transformation and inequality. It provides policymakers, researchers, and the public with an accessible view of how internet infrastructure distribution affects inclusivity in the digital economy.
