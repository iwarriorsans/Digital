Digital Divide Analysis

Project Description

With the rise of the digital economy, an important and evolving topic has been the digital divide. It was first granted international exposure and brought to the forefront when it began appearing in several United Nations reports, and has become a critical concern for organizations, policymakers, and scholars across various fields. Digital divide refers to the gap between people who have adequate access to information communication technology (ICT) and people who have poor or no access to ICT. The different forms of digital gaps can intensify inequalities within society as they can either restrict or improve citizens’ social and economic capital as well as their abilities to participate in society.
Note: this section is directly quoted from the introduction section of one journal article. Source. Needs further adjustment for later drafting.

Traditionally, much of the discourse around the digital divide has framed the issue in binary terms: the presence or absence of internet coverage (need more literature citation). While this perspective has been useful in establishing basic access goals, it obscures significant variations in the quality of internet access, including speed, reliability, and bandwidth. As scholarship has evolved, there is growing recognition that the digital divide is not only about whether people are connected, but how well they are connected. This distinction is essential, as poor-quality internet access can limit the developmental benefits typically associated with connectivity.

Despite this recognition, current empirical approaches remain constrained. Most existing metrics are area-based, measuring coverage over a geographic extent without accounting for the distribution of populations within those areas. This approach may obscure disparities in internet quality, particularly in densely populated but under-resourced communities. To address this gap, this report proposes a novel, population-based measurement index to capture the evolving nature of internet quality. By incorporating population distribution into internet performance metrics, this approach offers a more nuanced and equitable representation of digital inequality.

Beyond measurement, this report also explores the feasibility of predictive modeling techniques to fill existing data gaps. In regions where high-resolution internet speed data are unavailable or unreliable, can machine learning or neural network models generate accurate predictions based on alternative geospatial indicators, such as road networks or satellite-derived night-time lights? Furthermore, how robust are these predictions when validated across training and test sets? These secondary research questions aim to assess both the methodological rigor and the practical applicability of new measurement approaches to digital inequality.

Data Source

1. Data Source

This report utilizes three primary datasets to construct a population-based measurement of internet quality and assess digital inequality across space.

1.1 Internet Speed Data

(Copied from He’s previous note, could be adjusted in the future)

  • Source: Speedtest by Ookla – Global Fixed and Mobile Network Performance Maps
  • Description: Provides global fixed broadband and mobile (cellular) network performance metrics in zoom level 16 Web Mercator tiles (≈610m × 610m at the equator). Updated quarterly. On average, each quarter includes 4.9 million tiles for fixed internet data and 3.3 million tiles for mobile internet data. The dataset is aggregated from real-world user tests conducted via Ookla’s Speedtest application.
  • Access Link: Ookla Open Data

1.2 Population Density

  • Meta (shared by Sharada) – Provides population density estimates at a 30m × 30m resolution, derived from satellite imagery and census data.
  • Kontur (cleaned by Jinyun Huang) – Provides population estimates at 400m × 400m resolution, harmonized with administrative boundaries and prepared for integration.
  • Access Link: Kontur Population Data

1.3 Administrative Boundaries (Shapefiles)

  • Source: World Bank Internal System (shared by He)
  • Usage: Shapefiles for national and subnational boundaries are used to aggregate tile-level internet and population data to administrative levels (country, province). These boundaries also facilitate visualization and cross-country comparisons.

Methodology

Procedures

The original datasets provide average download/upload internet speed (kbps), latency (ms), number of tests, and number of devices performing the tests at the zoom-16 tile level for each quarter. The following steps outline the methodology for processing and aggregating the data to generate quarterly country-level internet speed estimates.

Understanding the Quadkey System

  • Tile Hierarchy: At zoom level 0, the entire world is represented by a single tile. Each zoom level subdivides every tile into four smaller tiles, exponentially increasing spatial resolution.
  • Quadkey Encoding: Each tile is uniquely identified by a quadkey, a string of digits where each digit (0–3) represents the tile’s position in a parent tile’s subdivision.
  • Zoom-16 vs. Zoom-14: At zoom-16, tiles are ~610.8m × 610.8m at the equator, while zoom-14 tiles cover ~2.5 km × 2.5 km, offering coarser but more computationally efficient resolution.

Example: Quadkey ‘123’ at Zoom Level 3

  • ‘1’ → Selects the upper-right tile at zoom-1.
  • ‘2’ → Chooses the lower-left tile within that upper-right tile.
  • ‘3’ → Picks the lower-right tile within the lower-left tile.

Step 1: Adjusting Spatial Granularity

The raw dataset is provided at zoom-16. To improve computational efficiency and ensure meaningful aggregation, granularity was reduced to zoom-14, where each tile represents ~2.5 km × 2.5 km.

Step 2: Computing Tile Centroids

Each zoom-14 tile is identified by a 14-digit quadkey. Centroid coordinates (latitude, longitude) are computed by:

  1. Decode the quadkey → Convert quadkey into tile coordinates (X, Y) and zoom level (Z) using base-4 encoding.
  2. Convert to Latitude/Longitude → Compute centroid in geographic coordinates (EPSG:4326).

Step 3: Assigning Internet Speed Data to Countries

Using the World Bank Official Boundaries dataset, a spatial lookup was performed to assign each zoom-14 tile centroid to the appropriate country.

Step 4: Aggregating Internet Speed at the Country/Admin1/Admin2 Level

  • Weighted averages: Internet speed and latency for mobile and fixed internet are averaged with number of tests as weights.
  • Weighted medians: Computed with number of devices as weights, using this procedure:
    1. Tiles within each country-year-quarter sorted by variable of interest (speed or latency).
    2. Cumulative sum of devices calculated.
    3. Tile at the 50th percentile identified → its value is the weighted median.

Output Variables

The final dataset provides quarterly country/admin1/admin2-level estimates of internet speed, including download/upload speeds, latency, and test volumes for both mobile and fixed internet connections.
